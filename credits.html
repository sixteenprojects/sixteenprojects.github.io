Founding Team

The people who started it and why they did

The Sixteen Project began with a conviction: the weight of cybersecurity cannot rest on a single pair of shoulders.

We are three practitioners who met across different corners of the security domain and found ourselves asking the same questions. My name is M. Daffi Alhafizh, and I started my career as a Security Analyst, learning to detect, triage, and respond to threats before the noise became signal. That grounding pushed me further, into threat hunting, chasing adversarial patterns buried deep in telemetry, and eventually into my current work as a Threat Researcher and Intelligence Analyst, where the focus shifts from finding threats to truly understanding them: their infrastructure, their motivations, and their tradecraft.

Along the way I recognized that the knowledge accumulated across each stage of this journey was too valuable to stay siloed. So I reached out to two colleagues who shared the same spirit. Rahmadini Farha Hayatunnufus brings the ability to surface intelligence from the murkiest corners of the internet, contextualizing threat actors and their operations beyond what surface-level data reveals. Muhammad Fadillah Fajari contributes the technical depth to dissect adversarial tooling and understand how malware behaves in the wild, from honeypot-captured samples to full reverse engineering.

Together we built The Sixteen Project, not as a product, but as a shared commitment: to make threat intelligence accessible, to document what we learn, and to give back to the community that made us better practitioners. Every report, every tool, every entry in the threat library is a piece of that commitment made tangible.

Why Collaboration?

Cyber resilience is not achieved by one person or one team — it is built by an ecosystem that shares, warns, and strengthens one another. The Sixteen Project believes that open knowledge is an active form of defense: the more people who understand the threat landscape, the harder it becomes for adversaries to operate in the shadows. Every analyst who shares a finding, every researcher who documents a technique, every practitioner who contributes to the community is performing an act of collective defense.
